violent_crime is defined as the sum of four offenses: homicide (homs_sum), rape (rape_sum), robbery (rob_sum), and aggravated assault (agg_ass_sum). These four categories follow the FBI’s Uniform Crime Reporting (UCR) definitions.

violent_per_100k is calculated as (violent_crime / total_pop) * 100000. This rate normalizes crime counts across cities of different population sizes and is the standard metric used in UCR comparisons.

months_reported indicates how many months of the year a city submitted crime data. If months_reported is less than 12, the crime totals may underrepresent the true annual count because missing months are not imputed in this dataset.

Homicide counts (homs_sum) include murder and non-negligent manslaughter but exclude negligent manslaughter and justifiable homicide, following UCR definitions.

Robbery (rob_sum) refers to taking or attempting to take anything of value from a person by force or threat of force. It is distinct from burglary, which involves unlawful entry into a structure; burglary is not included in violent_crime.

Aggravated assault (agg_ass_sum) includes attacks intended to inflict severe or aggravated bodily injury, typically involving a weapon or means likely to cause serious harm. Simple assault is not included in this dataset.

Rape (rape_sum) follows the UCR “revised definition,” which includes penetration without consent regardless of gender. This definition expanded in 2013, so comparisons across years should be interpreted cautiously.

The dataset includes 57 cities from 32 states. Cities vary widely in population size, which is why rate-based metrics (per 100k) are more reliable for comparison than raw crime counts.

Some cities have missing geographic coordinates (lat, lng). When coordinates are missing, those cities will not appear on the map but still appear in tables and charts.

Crime rates can fluctuate due to population changes. A city with a declining population may show an increased violent_per_100k even if the number of crimes stays constant.

Cities with fewer than 12 months_reported should not be compared directly to cities with full-year reporting when analyzing trends or ranking cities by crime rate.

The FBI UCR program transitioned to NIBRS reporting in 2021, which changed how some offenses are categorized. This dataset uses UCR-style summary reporting, so comparisons to NIBRS-based sources may differ.
